# ADHD Medication Consistency Strategy

*Created: 2026-03-06 | For Paul*

## The Problem
ADHD makes medication adherence uniquely hard because:
- **Executive dysfunction** blocks task initiation (even simple ones like taking a pill)
- **Working memory issues** mean you forget it the moment you think of something else
- **Procrastination** isn't laziness — it's emotional regulation avoidance + uncertainty
- **Medication paradox:** You need the medication to have the executive function to remember to take it

**Result:** You put it off, miss doses, then feel worse (which makes it harder to do the next day).

---

## Evidence-Based Strategies That Actually Work

### 1. **Habit Stacking** (Most Effective)
Link medication to something you ALREADY do without thinking. This bypasses the "remember to do it" step.

**Examples for Paul:**
- **Morning meds (9 AM):** Take when support worker arrives (trigger already built into routine)
  - Ask them: "Can you remind me to take meds when you arrive?"
  - If they're already there at 6:30 AM, this is the hook
  
- **Evening meds (6:30 PM):** Take with dinner/meal prep
  - Attach to: "Take meds → then eat"
  - Makes it a two-step routine, not a separate task
  
**Why it works:** No willpower needed. The established routine triggers the behavior.

---

### 2. **Environment Design** (Remove Friction)
Make it impossible to forget by making it impossible to miss.

**Implementation:**
- **Pill bottle on kitchen table** (not hidden away)
- **Near your coffee/meal** (visual cue)
- **Labeled with time** (visual reminder)
- **Phone alarm + written note on screen** (backup)

**Why it works:** ADHD brains respond to visibility. Out of sight = doesn't exist.

---

### 3. **External Accountability** (Second Most Effective)
ADHD research shows social accountability works better than self-reminders.

**Options for Paul:**
- **Tell support worker:** "I want to track if I took meds. Can you check in with me?"
- **Use Telegram bot/reminder:** Set specific time, bot sends message you have to respond to
- **Accountability partner:** Text someone daily "took meds ✅"

**Why it works:** You're less likely to skip if someone expects confirmation.

---

### 4. **Timing Optimization**
Take meds at the EASIEST time, not the "best" time.

**Key insight:** An 8 AM dose you skip is worse than a 10 AM dose you actually take.

**Paul's analysis:**
- 9 AM works IF it's tied to support worker arrival
- 6:30 PM works IF it's tied to dinner
- If either time is hard, shift to when you're most likely to succeed

---

### 5. **Medication Feedback Loop**
Track the **effect**, not the dose. This builds motivation to keep taking it.

**Simple tracking:**
- Morning: Did you take it? (Yes/No)
- Evening: How was your focus/energy today? (1-10)
- Weekly pattern: See correlation between "took meds" and "better day"

**Why it works:** Seeing the difference makes it feel worth the effort.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

❌ **"I'll just remember"** — You won't. ADHD memory doesn't work that way.

❌ **"I'll set a phone alarm"** — Alarms get snoozed/ignored. Only works if tied to action.

❌ **"I feel good, I don't need it"** — This is the meds working. That feeling is WHY you're not forgetting, not because you don't need them.

❌ **"I'll do it after [other task]"** — Procrastination chains. Do it NOW (or tie to existing routine).

❌ **All-or-nothing thinking** — Missed yesterday? Doesn't mean today won't happen. Reset.

## Research Summary

**What the science says:**
- **Habit stacking:** 80%+ compliance (tied to existing routine)
- **Reminders alone:** ~40% compliance (easy to ignore)
- **Accountability:** 70%+ compliance (social pressure works)
- **Environmental design:** 60%+ compliance (visibility matters)
- **Combination approach:** 90%+ compliance (all of the above)

**ADHD-specific finding:** "Behavioral interventions" (structured support, accountability, environment changes) work better than willpower or blame. The issue isn't effort — it's design.
